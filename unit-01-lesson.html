📖 ESSENTIAL QUESTION
An artist has only seven building blocks to work with. How do line, shape, form, space, value, color, and texture turn a blank surface into a place you can almost step into?
This unit gives you the names for everything you will use for the rest of the year. Learn the seven elements once, and you can read any artwork and build your own on purpose instead of by accident.
👀 PART 1 · LOOK FIRST

Before we name anything, just look. Spend a full minute with the painting below. Let your eye wander the way it wants to.

This is Van Gogh's own bedroom in the south of France. He painted it three times because the room mattered to him: it was the first place he had ever truly owned. Notice how much he tells you with so little. The floor tilts up toward you. The bed is heavy and solid. The walls are a restless blue. Two of everything sits waiting, as if for a guest who has not arrived.

💬 WONDER FIRST
What do you notice first in this room? What feels a little off about how it is built? Hold your answer in mind. By the end of this lesson you will have the vocabulary to say exactly what Van Gogh did.
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN · NO WRONG ANSWER
When you look at this bedroom, which feeling comes closest?

Every one of those answers can be defended from the painting itself. That is the point: an artist builds a feeling out of choices you can point to. Now learn to name those choices.

📚 PART 2 · NAME WHAT YOU SEE

The Elements of Art are the seven basic tools every artist uses, in every culture and every century. They are the grammar of art: the way letters build words, the elements build images.

LineA path made by a moving point. Its weight and direction carry feeling. Van Gogh's lines almost vibrate.
ShapeA flat, enclosed area. Geometric shapes are precise; organic shapes are free, like those in nature.
FormShape with the illusion of depth: height, width, and volume. Value turns a flat circle into a ball.
SpaceThe area within, around, and between things. Overlap and size make a flat surface feel deep.
ValueHow light or dark something is. Value is how artists fake light, shadow, and depth on flat paper.
ColorMade by light. Three traits: hue (its name), value (light or dark), and intensity (bright or dull).
TextureHow a surface feels, or looks like it would feel. Real (you can touch it) or implied (painted to fool the eye).
💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
Three of these elements are the secret to making flat paper look three-dimensional: value (light and shadow), space (overlap and size), and form (the illusion of volume). You will practice all three this quarter, starting with your dream room.

Value: the workhorse element

Of the seven, value does the most heavy lifting. A value scale runs from the lightest light to the darkest dark, with even steps in between. Squint at any photograph and color drops away, but the values remain. That is why a black-and-white photo still reads perfectly.

A 7-step value scale, lightest to darkest. You will draw your own in the Week 1 sketch.

No pencil touched this crater. Low, raking sunlight did all the work: the rim catches full light, the far wall drops to a value near pure black, and the slopes in between step through every value on the scale above. This is value's real job, on the moon or on your paper: it is the only element that can turn a flat circle into a hole in the ground, or a flat shape into a bed you could lie down in.

🔍 PART 3 · WATCH IT WORK

Artists and critics look at art in a deliberate order, so that judgment is earned and not just blurted. The four steps spell DAIJ: Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Judge. Watch it applied to Van Gogh's The Bedroom. You will use the same steps in your VR critique this week.

Describe (just the facts)

A small room. A wooden bed with red blankets. Two chairs, two pillows, a window, framed pictures on a blue wall, a washstand. The floor is a tilted reddish-brown.

Analyze (how is it built?)

Bold dark lines outline every object. The floorboards rush toward the window so the room feels deep but also tips toward us: that is space at work. Large, simple shapes keep it calm, and the blue walls set off the warm reds and yellows, a deliberate color contrast.

Interpret (what is it saying?)

Van Gogh said he wanted the room to express rest. Yet the tilting floor and the empty second chair can read as loneliness. Both are fair, because the evidence supports both.

Judge (does it succeed?)

It succeeds: with seven simple elements and no fancy detail, Van Gogh built a room you feel before you can explain. That is the goal of this whole course.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE ELEMENT
Read each prompt, commit to an answer, then check it.
The floorboards rush toward the window, making the room feel deep. Which element is Van Gogh leaning on most?
The bedposts look round and solid, lighter on one side and darker on the other. Which element does that?
▶  Try it in your notebook: name one more place Van Gogh uses VALUE, then reveal a check.
Write your answer before you open this. One strong answer: the shadow under the bed, and the darker washstand drawer fronts, where value separates one object from the next and grounds them in the room.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND YOUR DREAM ROOM
An AI image generator can produce a picture of a "dream room" from a text prompt in seconds, and it can be a quick way to gather ideas or moods. It cannot make the seven decisions this project is teaching you to make: where your light comes from, which shapes carry the eye, how value builds depth. Those choices, and the drawing itself, are the work being graded here.

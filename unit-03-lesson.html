📖 ESSENTIAL QUESTION
A single small shape, repeated and turned with care, can cover a whole wall and seem to go on forever. How do pattern, balance, and symmetry turn one motif into something that feels endless?
Last unit you learned the seven elements of art. This unit you put them to work with three principles of design: pattern, balance, and symmetry. Islamic geometric ornament is one of the clearest places in all of art to study them, because the whole design is built from these three ideas.
👀 PART 1 · LOOK FIRST

Before we name anything, just look. Spend a full minute with the tile below. Let your eye travel across it and notice where it wants to go.

This tile was made in Iznik, a town in Ottoman Turkey, around 1545 A.D. A single tile like this was one unit in a much larger wall. Set side by side, dozens of identical tiles built a surface that repeats across a room. Notice what is here and what is not. There are no people and no animals. Instead the design is made of curving leaves and flowers, arranged so the shapes balance and flow. The same kinds of shapes return again and again.

💬 WONDER FIRST
Where does your eye go when you look at this tile? Does it settle on one spot, or does it keep moving? Hold your answer in mind. By the end of this lesson you will have the vocabulary to say exactly how this design pulls your eye the way it does.
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN · NO WRONG ANSWER
When you look at this tile, which feeling comes closest?

Every one of those answers can be defended from the tile itself. That is the point: the artisans built that feeling out of choices you can point to. Now learn to name those choices.

📚 PART 2 · NAME WHAT YOU SEE

The Principles of Design are ways of arranging the elements so a whole surface holds together. Three of them carry this unit: pattern, balance, and symmetry. Across many Islamic cultures and periods, artists favored geometric and plant-based (vegetal) ornament, and in religious settings they generally avoided images of people and animals. That choice pushed the art toward exactly these three principles.

PatternA motif repeated in a planned way. When copies fit edge to edge with no gaps, that is a tessellation, and it can cover any surface as an allover pattern.
BalanceVisual weight shared evenly. In radial balance the design spreads from a center point, the same in every direction, like spokes on a wheel.
SymmetryOne side mirrors the other across a line or a center. Fold the design along that line and the two halves match.
💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
These three ideas work together. A single motif is repeated to make a pattern; the repeats are arranged with symmetry so the halves mirror; and the whole spreads from a center in radial balance. Get the geometry right and the pieces lock together like a puzzle that could keep growing.

The hidden grid: where the stars come from

These designs are not drawn freehand. Underneath a geometric Islamic pattern is a grid built with only two tools: a compass to draw circles and a straightedge to draw straight lines. Artisans called the underlying line framework girih, the family of star-and-polygon designs. From a few overlapping circles you can find the points for a perfect star. Watch one grow, step by step.

1. CirclesSet the compass and step it around. Overlapping circles mark where the lines will cross.
2. Guide linesA straightedge connects the crossing points, dividing the circle into equal slices.
3. The starTrace selected lines and an 8-point star appears, ready to repeat across the whole surface.

That third star is the kind of motif you will build in your own tile this cycle. Drawn once with care, then repeated and turned, it becomes an allover pattern.

This is a real wall, not a diagram. At the Alhambra, a Nasrid palace complex in Granada, Spain, carved and tiled ornament repeats the same compass-and-straightedge logic you just watched built step by step above. The star-and-polygon grid is not a classroom invention. It is how walls like this one were actually laid out, centuries ago, with nothing but a compass and a straightedge.

🔍 PART 3 · WATCH IT WORK

Artists and critics look at art in a deliberate order, so that judgment is earned and not just blurted. The four steps spell DAIJ: Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Judge. Watch it applied to the Iznik tile above. You will use the same steps in your VR critique this week.

Describe (just the facts)

A square glazed tile in blues and turquoise on a white ground. A long, curving leaf sweeps across it, with smaller blossoms and stems around it. The shapes are outlined and filled flatly under a clear glaze. There are no people or animals.

Analyze (how is it built?)

The tile is one motif meant to repeat into an allover pattern when many tiles are set together. The curving leaf and its blossoms are arranged so the visual weight stays in balance across the square. Echoing shapes on either side give it a near-mirror symmetry, and flowing lines tie the parts into one continuous movement.

Interpret (what is it saying?)

Because the design favors plant and geometric forms rather than figures, the eye reads it as ordered and endless rather than as a scene to read. In many Islamic settings this repeating, edgeless ornament was understood to suggest the infinite and the unity behind all things.

Judge (does it succeed?)

It succeeds: with a few shapes and strict arrangement, the artisans made one tile that feels complete on its own and also built to repeat without end. That balance of order and continuity is the goal of the design.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND GEOMETRIC PATTERN
An AI image generator can produce a picture of an "Islamic geometric pattern" in seconds, and it can be a quick way to gather color ideas or moods. Look closely, though, and the stars often do not line up and the repeats break, because the tool imitates the look without building the geometry. It cannot teach you the compass-and-straightedge construction that makes a real pattern lock together and repeat without gaps. That construction is the skill this project is teaching, and it is the part that comes from your own hand and your own reasoning.
📝 VOCABULARY
PatternA motif repeated in a planned, regular way across a surface.
MotifThe single repeated unit or design that builds a pattern.
TessellationShapes that fit together edge to edge with no gaps or overlaps.
Allover PatternA pattern that repeats evenly across an entire surface.
BalanceThe even distribution of visual weight in a design.
Radial BalanceBalance that spreads out from a central point in all directions.
SymmetryOne side mirrors the other across a line or center.
GirihThe framework of straight lines used to build a star-and-polygon design.
ArabesqueFlowing, scrolling plant-based (vegetal) ornament common in Islamic art.
Compass & StraightedgeThe two tools used to construct the grid behind a geometric pattern.

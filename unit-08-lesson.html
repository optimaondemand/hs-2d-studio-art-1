📖 ESSENTIAL QUESTION
A sculptor works in real volume you can walk around. You work on flat paper. How does value let you fake that volume so convincingly that a drawn shape seems to swell out toward you?
In Unit 1 you met form as one of the seven elements. This unit puts it to work. You will learn how a flat shape becomes a believable form, study how Greek sculptors built volume in stone, and end by rendering a single water droplet so round and wet it looks ready to roll off the page.
👀 PART 1 · LOOK FIRST

Before we name anything, just look. Spend a full minute with the figure below. Imagine walking a slow circle around it.

This is a kouros, a standing youth carved in Greece roughly 2,600 years ago. It is not a picture of a person. It is a real object with weight and bulk, a thing you could circle and touch. The sculptor did not draw the light and shadow you see on it. The stone itself catches real light, and that light falls across rounded chest, thigh, and shoulder to reveal solid volume from every side.

💭 QUICK CHECK-IN · NO WRONG ANSWER

Each of those answers points at the same idea from a different angle: this object has volume, and light reveals it. Your job this unit is to produce that same read on flat paper, using only value.

📚 PART 2 · NAME WHAT YOU SEE

A shape is flat: it has only height and width, like a circle drawn on paper. A form has a third dimension, depth, so it owns height, width, and the bulk that fills the space inside it. A sculptor works in real form. A painter or a draftsman creates the illusion of form on a flat surface. The kouros is real form. The drawing you make this week will be illusion.

Volume and mass

Two words describe what a form takes up. Volume is the amount of space a form occupies, the sense that it is full inside rather than hollow or flat. Mass is the visual weight and bulk of that form, how heavy and solid it looks. The kouros has obvious mass: it reads as a heavy block of stone, not a paper cutout.

The four basic solids

Almost every form, from a fruit to a face to a Greek torso, is built from four geometric solids working together. Learn to see these four and you can shade anything.

SphereA ball. The light wraps around it in a smooth, curving gradient. No hard edges anywhere on its surface.
CubeA box. Each flat plane catches a different amount of light, so values jump suddenly at the crisp edges.
ConeA curved side that shades like a sphere, narrowing to a point. The base reads as a flat ellipse.
CylinderA can or a column. The curved wall shades left to right like a sphere; the top is a flat ellipse.
💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
Curved solids (sphere, cone, cylinder) shade with a smooth, gradual gradient, because the surface turns away from the light little by little. Flat-sided solids (the cube) shade with sudden value jumps at each edge, because each plane meets the light at its own fixed angle. Reading that difference is most of the skill.

How value turns a shape into a form

Pick one light source. Wherever the surface faces the light, leave it bright. Wherever it turns away, darken it. The flat circle on the left is just a shape. The same circle on the right, given a light source and a value gradient, becomes a sphere. Nothing was added but value.

Five things appear on a well-shaded sphere: the bright highlight where light hits directly, the lit midtones, the curved core shadow where it turns away, the warmer reflected light bouncing back from the surface below, and the cast shadow the ball throws onto that surface.

The same five effects, in a real kitchen egg

The diagrams above are built from clean gradients so the logic is easy to see. Here is the same logic caught by a camera, not a computer, in an object you have handled a hundred times.

Find all five effects in the photograph before you move on: the small bright highlight where the overhead light lands most directly, the lit midtone beside it, the core shadow where the shell curves fully away from the light, a faint warmth of reflected light along the shadow edge, and the soft cast shadow spreading on the table. An egg is not a perfect sphere, but it is shaded by exactly the same rule you just practiced on the diagrams. This is precisely what you will go hunting for on your own water droplet.

Two ways to sculpt: relief and freestanding

Greek sculptors worked in two modes. Relief sculpture stays attached to a background slab and projects partway out, like a coin or a carved temple panel; you view it from the front. Freestanding (or in-the-round) sculpture is fully detached, like the kouros, so you can circle it and read its form from every side. The kouros is freestanding, which is exactly why it teaches form so well.

🔍 PART 3 · WATCH IT WORK

Recall the four-step critique order, DAIJ: Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Judge. Watch it applied to the marble kouros, with our eye fixed on how the sculptor builds three-dimensional form and volume. You will run the same steps in your VR sculpture critique this week, on a sculpture you choose yourself.

Describe (just the facts)

A life-size marble youth stands upright. Arms hang straight at the sides, fists closed. One leg steps slightly forward. The hair falls in beaded rows down the back. The figure is bare and symmetrical.

Analyze (how is it built?)

The sculptor treats the body as a set of clear solids. The torso reads as two rounded masses, the chest above and the abdomen below. Arms and the forward leg are firm cylinders. The head is close to a sphere. Because the carving rounds every surface, light slides across each mass in a smooth gradient, so you read full volume from any angle. The pose is rigid and frontal, typical of the earliest Greek figures.

Interpret (what is it saying?)

The calm, upright stance and idealized body suggest permanence and order, a youth made to stand for the ages. The Greek culture of this period prized balance and the human form as a measure of beauty.

Judge (does it succeed?)

It succeeds at the thing this unit is about. By organizing the body into clean rounded solids, the unnamed Attic sculptor gave the figure undeniable volume and mass. You believe a real body stands inside the stone, and that is the illusion you will chase on paper.

An AI tool can spin a 3-D model of a sphere or a droplet on screen and relight it from any angle in a second. It cannot teach your eye to read light as it travels across a curved form, and it cannot teach your hand to lay down a value gradient that turns a flat shape into a believable one. That eye-to-hand skill is what this droplet drawing builds, and it is what is being graded.
📝 VOCABULARY
FormA three-dimensional element: height, width, and depth (volume).
ShapeA flat, two-dimensional area: only height and width.
VolumeThe amount of space a form occupies; its sense of fullness.
MassThe visual weight and solid bulk of a form.
Geometric solidsThe four basic forms: sphere, cube, cone, cylinder.
ValueThe lightness or darkness of a surface; what makes form on flat paper.
HighlightThe brightest spot, where light hits a form most directly.
Core shadowThe darker band where a curved surface turns from the light.
Cast shadowThe dark shape a form throws onto a nearby surface.
ReliefSculpture that projects from a background slab; viewed from the front.
FreestandingDetached sculpture viewable from all sides; also called in-the-round.

💪 THE BIG IDEA

A Greek sculptor builds volume in real stone. You build the illusion of it with value on flat paper. Both come down to one move: decide where the light is, then let the surface darken as it turns away. Master that on a sphere, and a water droplet, a face, or a marble torso all become possible.

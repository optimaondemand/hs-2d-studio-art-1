📖 ESSENTIAL QUESTION
A drawing is flat and a sky is not solid. So how does an artist make a surface look rough, soft, ridged, or fuzzy when there is nothing under your finger but paper?
This unit is about texture, the element that tells your eye how a surface would feel. You will learn the difference between texture you can actually touch and texture that is only an illusion, and you will build a toolkit of marks that fake any surface you want.
👀 PART 1 · LOOK FIRST

Before we name anything, just look. Spend a full minute with the painting below. Imagine running a finger across the sky. What would it feel like?

Van Gogh painted this from a window at an asylum in the south of France, mostly from memory. Look closely at the sky. It is not smooth at all. It is built from short, curling ribbons of thick paint that twist around the stars like water moving down a river. The paint sits up off the canvas in visible ridges. If you stood in front of the real painting in New York, you could see the shadows those ridges cast.

💬 WONDER FIRST
The sky in this painting has two textures at once. There is the texture you would feel if you touched the canvas, and there is the texture your eye reads as swirling, churning air. Hold that idea. By the end of this lesson you will have a name for each one.
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN · NO WRONG ANSWER
If you could touch the surface of this painting, which word fits best?

Most of those answers describe the real, touchable surface. But the painting also makes you feel a churning, windy sky that is not really there. That gap between what you could touch and what your eye sees is exactly what this unit is about.

📚 PART 2 · NAME WHAT YOU SEE

Texture is the element of art that describes how a surface feels, or how it looks like it would feel. It comes in two kinds, and good artists use both on purpose.

Actual textureThe real, physical feel of a surface. Thick paint, a rough canvas, or layered material you could touch with a finger.
Implied textureTexture that is only an illusion. The paper stays flat, but marks trick your eye into seeing fur, bark, or stone.

When a painter loads the brush with thick paint so it stands up off the surface, that built-up ridge is called impasto. Van Gogh is famous for it. His skies and sunflowers carry real, touchable texture because the paint itself is sculpted. Impasto is one way to get actual texture.

In a pencil or pen drawing you cannot pile up material that way. Instead you fake the feel of a surface with repeated marks. This is called mark-making, and it is the heart of your project this cycle. Here are four of the most useful patterns.

HatchingA set of parallel lines all running the same way. Closer lines read as darker and denser.
Cross-hatchingTwo or more layers of hatching that cross. Each added layer deepens the shadow and the texture.
StipplingTexture built only from dots. More dots packed together read as darker; spread out, they read as light.
ScribblingLoose, tangled, looping lines. Great for matted fur, foliage, wool, or anything irregular and soft.
💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
All four of these marks control value as well as texture. Pack the marks tighter and the area grows darker; spread them out and it lightens. That is why a single technique like stippling can give you both the dark and the light of a surface. Texture and value travel together.

One subject, many textures

The fastest way to grow your skill is to put the marks side by side and compare. The strip below shows four textures from the same small toolkit. Notice that nothing here is colored or shaded by hand. Every shift in feel comes from the kind of mark and how densely it is packed.

Even hatching reads as a flat, woven or grassy surface.
Cross-hatching reads as a denser, harder, shadowed surface.
Stippling reads as grainy, sandy, or sponge-like.
Scattered marks read as soft, mottled, or fuzzy.
🔍 PART 3 · WATCH IT WORK

Artists and critics look at art in a deliberate order, so that judgment is earned and not just blurted. The four steps spell DAIJ: Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Judge. Watch it applied to Van Gogh's The Starry Night, with our eye on texture. You will use the same steps in your VR critique this week.

Describe (just the facts)

A night sky over a small town. Eleven large, glowing stars and a bright crescent moon. A dark, flame-shaped tree rises along the left edge. A church spire points up from the village below. The whole sky moves in long, curling bands.

Analyze (how is it built?)

The sky is built from short curved strokes of impasto, paint laid on so thick it stands up off the canvas as actual texture. Those same swirling strokes also create an implied texture of churning, windy air. The cypress tree is made of long upward licks of paint, while the village uses shorter, calmer dabs. The contrast between the restless sky and the still town is built almost entirely from how the marks move.

Interpret (what is it saying?)

The heavy, moving paint can read as a sky that is alive, full of energy and feeling, looming over a quiet, sleeping town. Some viewers read it as wonder, others as turmoil. Both are fair, because the brushwork supports both.

Judge (does it succeed?)

It succeeds. The texture is not decoration. The thick, moving paint is the feeling. You sense the energy of that sky before you can explain it, and that is exactly what texture, used on purpose, can do.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND TEXTURE
An image app can apply a "brushstroke" or "oil paint" filter to a photo in one tap, and it can imitate the look of impasto on screen. It cannot train your hand to build texture mark by mark, deciding where the fur is dense and where it thins, where a surface is rough and where it is smooth. Those decisions, and the drawing you make from them, are the skill being practiced and graded here.
📝 VOCABULARY
TextureHow a surface feels, or appears it would feel.
Actual textureThe real, physical feel of a surface you could touch.
Implied textureAn illusion of texture on a flat surface, made with marks.
ImpastoPaint laid on thick enough to stand up off the surface as real texture.
Mark-makingThe marks an artist uses to build texture, value, and surface.
HatchingParallel lines running the same direction.
Cross-hatchingLayers of hatching that cross to deepen value and texture.
StipplingTexture and value built entirely from dots.
Post-ImpressionismA movement after Impressionism, including Van Gogh, that used bold color and visible, expressive brushwork.
DAIJThe critique order: Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Judge.

💪 THE BIG IDEA

Texture is how a flat picture reaches out and tells your fingers what it would feel like. Van Gogh built his with real, thick paint. You will build yours with marks: hatching, cross-hatching, stippling, and scribbles. This week, you turn a handful of marks into fur, scales, and feathers.
